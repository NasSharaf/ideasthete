Fake News and Incentives Based Thinking

The Rashomon Effect and Betting Markets

The Rashomon effect is described as the contradictory interpretations of an event by different people―the same event is interpreted completely differently by different people, even though they have witnessed the same incident. — Psychologenie

About a year ago today, as Facebook has so kindly reminded me, I had an idea for news betting exchange. I’ve somewhat given up on the idea in the short-term, but I wanted to lay out my thought process so that you might get something from it. If nothing else, I hope that the way I think about these ideas gives you some inkling as to how you might be able to think about other problems.

In Freakonomics, Steven Dubner stresses that the fundamental idea to economics is incentive based thinking and that economics is actually just the study of incentives. What he means is actually pretty simple — think about what incentives are at play, for every party involved, and you now think like an economist.

Initially I got interested in economics as a misguided attempt to ‘adult’ better and figure out finances and stocks and things like that. But pretty soon, I started to like this way of thinking, as for some reason it complemented the engineering half of my brain. And whenever you discover a new tool, you tend to try to use it everywhere, right? When all you have is a hammer, every problem looks like a nail.

It being 2017 at the time, I applied this line of logic to the issue of “Fake News.” First, what are the incentives for the news consumer? Most consumers would probably say they keep up with the news to stay informed. But this is actually not true. People keep up with the news because:

  1. Gather evidence to support their worldview. This leads to confirmation bias.
  2. Impress people they admire or think are smart that they, the consumer, are smart. This leads to filter bubbles.
  3. Signal to people they admire or think are smart that they, the consumer, share common ground. This leads to echo chambers.

That’s one end. The other end is the media companies. They would probably say that their incentive is their civic duty to shine a light on the ills of society. But really, their incentives are:

  1. Survival. Many papers are simply trying to adapt to a quick-changing landscape. Many media companies are looking for a sustainable business model in this information age because print doesn’t sell and competitors will often give content for free, setting a market price of $0.
  2. Profit. This might mean subscriptions (but that means paywalls) or it could mean advertisements. Trying to get eyeballs on ads generally means that papers resort to ‘viral’ techniques and in general sub-par journalism.
  3. Look like they have integrity. It might sound like I’m being cynical, but I don’t mean it in a bad way. The newspapers want to be relevant and trusted so that they can convert that into subscriptions, so they have to earn that trust in some way or at least look like they deserve that trust.

So what does all of this mean?

Quite simply, the reason fake news was so prevalent was because all of the incentives were misaligned. The consumer end was incentivized to become more tribal, polarize more, and seek out what confirms their worldview. Media organizations are desperate enough to go along with this because that generates enough ad revenue to get by. Neither side is incentivized to try to figure out the truth.

This seems disheartening, but understanding a problem means that there is greater understanding of how to solve that problem. If a system of misaligned incentives or disincentives are collapsing our idea of truth, then a system of properly aligned incentives should help us build an idea of collective truth.

Another mental model I was mulling over was Elon Musk’s First Principals thinking. The idea in First Principles thinking is to start from the very fundamental building blocks, the absolutely smallest minutia you know to be true and build up a solution from there. We’ve kind of done that already with this incentives based analysis. But the other thing Elon Musk has added to this is to ‘try to imagine…I don’t want to say utopia, but…try to imagine a better future.’ Let’s imagine what the ideal solution would be then.

Elon explaining First Principles thinking

Ideally, if I could, I would simply pay everyone to read as much of the news as possible equally. Everyone gets paid to read everything — Fox, CNN, Infowars, Huffington Post, all of it. My thought here is that the only thing that would get people over this disincentive to only chase the news they want would be if you paid people to give an equal chance to the news they don’t like. But I’m not remotely wealth enough to do that.

But the next best thing would be to offer the opportunity to get paid for reading something you potentially disagree with. A bet. And in fact, betting on whether you think the news is fake or not is a much better solution, because it forces you to have what Nassim Nicholas Taleb calls “skin in the game.” You will be much more hesitant to call something fake news unless you were sure it was fake news.

Think to children, who often when a friend is making exaggerated claims will say “I’ll bet you didn’t” or “I’ll bet you did”. Generally, this is the child’s way of testing social claims — dare the opposing party to perform and put a stake on it. Pretty simple.

“His argument for these is pretty simple: they [prediction markets] ensure that we have a financial stake in being accurate when we make forecasts, rather than just trying to look good to our peers.” — Nate Silver, the Signal and the Noise

But what about the media organizations? How does news consumers betting on their content help a news organization? Best way is in a betting exchange, the exchange earns a commission on each bet. Betting exchanges help facilitate setting up the bet, maintain fair odds (fairer than a bookie at least), and if ran with the intent to supply news companies with a commission, could eliminate this clickbait, ad-driven business model that’s destroying the idea of truth.

Well, supposing you buy this logic, the question now is how do we do this? Imagine if one receives anonymized, randomized articles (without giveaways to the channel, author, etc) and is forced at the end to determine whether it is Democratic or Republican. Or sponsored content or genuine reporting. Or even whether the article is from a joke news site or not.

But how do you know if you are correct? Everyone will dispute the truth of an event or thought piece. This is true, but it is much harder to dispute the political leanings of a publication on a relative scale to other publications. I think most will agree that the New Revolution is nowhere near as left as Antifa News. It is about where the article falls on a relative scale compared to its peers, so to speak. Sometimes I’ve heard this called the Overton window (its not what it really is, but its still interesting.)

What we are really trying to deal with is uncertainty. I’m not certain about the quality of my news. Most Americans are uncertain about the quality of their news. Bets are market-based solutions to deal with uncertainty.

Think of it like this, if I said, “I’ll bet you the sky is blue”, would you take that? Probably not, because we know that the sky is blue. This generally leads people to believe that placing a bet means that we are betting on an event and not a state, like in prediction markets. Who will be the next president? What will natural gas go for in two years? Will Britain leave the EU? These are events that neither party know the outcome to with binary results.

My point is simple. We shouldn’t limit our bets to future events. Time is not what we are betting on — we are betting on uncertainty. I won’t take the “Is the sky blue?” bet because I am certain it is. I am not certain, however, whether the media is accurately reporting or not.

I have called this idea the “Rashomon idea” because what we are effectively dealing with in our Fake News crisis is competing ideas and views about how the world works. In Akira Kurosawa’s Rashomon, the Japanese court must deal with four competing accounts of a murder near the Rashomon city gate. The movie deals with issues we are dealing with now. Namely, how can we reconcile mutually contradictory accounts of the same events when it is in all men’s self-interest to bend the narrative for himself?

A news betting market would hold people accountable and use the inherent self-interest of people towards building some truth. Those who are accurate and perceptive get rewarded and can be looked to as guides in this uncertain landscape. Those who are wrong? Well…
